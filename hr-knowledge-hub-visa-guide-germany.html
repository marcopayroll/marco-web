Types of Work Visas

German immigration matters are jointly administered by the Federal Ministry of the Interior (BMI) and the Federal Foreign Office (AA). Labour-market review is handled by the Federal Employment Agency (Bundesagentur für Arbeit, BA), while residence permits are issued by the local foreigner authority (Ausländerbehörde). From November 2023 to June 2024, the Skilled Workers Immigration Act (Fachkräfteeinwanderungsgesetz) was fully reformed in three stages, with several new rules taking effect from 2026. The following are the main work-related residence permits for non-EU / EEA / Swiss nationals.

Residence Permit TypeWho It Applies ToDescription (2026)
EU Blue Card (Blaue Karte EU, § 18g)Highly qualified professionalsMain high-skilled talent pathway. General occupations: annual salary >= €50,700; shortage occupations (IT / engineering / natural sciences / healthcare, etc.): annual salary >= €45,934.20; degree or equivalent qualification required; issued for 4 years; permanent residence possible after 21–27 months.
Skilled worker visa (Fachkraft, § 18a / 18b)Academic and vocational skilled workersRecognized bachelor / master degree or >= 2-year vocational training; job does not need to strictly match the qualification; salary must meet local industry level; valid for up to 4 years.

The EU Blue Card (Blaue Karte EU) and the skilled worker visa (§ 18b) are the most common routes for employees going to Germany. The Blue Card accounts for more than 60% of high-skilled foreign employee visas. The following sections focus on the Blue Card while also covering the skilled worker visa and Anerkennungspartnerschaft.

Applicant Requirements

1. Employee-side Requirements

(1) EU Blue Card (Blaue Karte EU)

Salary threshold (from 2026.1.1): general occupations >= €50,700; shortage occupations >= €45,934.20 (including IT, engineering, natural sciences, mathematics, healthcare, etc.).
Education: German-recognized bachelor or master degree; or home-country degree with ZAB / anabin database recognition.
The job does not need to strictly match the degree after the 2023.11 reform.
Employment contract term >= 6 months.
Special route for IT specialists: >= 3 years professional experience + shortage-occupation salary threshold → degree exemption.
No mandatory German-language requirement; however, A1 / B1 can shorten the permanent residence timeline (27 months / 21 months).

(2) Skilled worker visa (§ 18a / 18b)

Degree or >= 2-year vocational training qualification, recognized by ZAB / chamber of commerce or industry authority.
The job does not need to strictly match the training / degree.
Salary must meet the local German industry level, generally no lower than collective-agreement standards.
Applicants aged >= 45 usually need additional pension-security proof, such as annual salary >= €53,130 or equivalent private pension.

(3) Professional experience route (§ 19c (2))

Non-academic vocational training or a qualification recognized by the home-country government.
>= 2 years relevant professional experience within the last 5 years.
BA approval required; salary and working hours must meet local standards; 2026 salary >= €43,470.
IT specialists may be exempt from any formal qualification proof and assessed by experience only.

(4) Recognition partnership (Anerkennungspartnerschaft)

A2 German (CEFR).
Academic or vocational qualification obtained abroad, but German Anerkennung not yet completed.
Employer signs a written commitment to support qualification recognition within 3 years.
Salary and job conditions must meet local or collective-agreement level.

2. Employer-side Requirements

The company must be registered in Germany (HRB commercial register) and have legal tax and social security contribution records.
Provide an employment contract with conditions equivalent to local employees, including salary, working hours, leave, and social security.
General occupations / § 19c routes require BA approval. Blue Card shortage occupations and Fachkraft academic routes may also involve BA review; Blue Card general occupations usually do not require BA consent but still require filing.
From 2026.1.1, new information duty: before the employee’s first working day, the employer must inform non-EU employees in writing of their right to use the free “Faire Integration” labour and social-law advisory service and indicate the nearest advisory point.
Any employee change, including salary / position / termination, must be notified to the Ausländerbehörde in time.
For Anerkennungspartnerschaft, the employer must prove vocational training capability or existing training experience.

Employer Written Information Duty from 2026.1.1

From 2026.1.1, employers have a new written information duty (Hinweispflicht). Failure to comply may be treated as a violation of the Residence Act and may trigger corporate hiring-compliance review. The new rule applies only to non-EU employees newly hired after 2026.1.1; existing employees do not need retroactive processing, but employers are advised to include it in the onboarding process.

Application Document Checklist

1. Employee Documents

Valid passport (remaining validity >= 6 months) + scan of personal information page.
Biometric ID photo (35 × 45mm, Schengen standard).
Employment contract / job offer, including position, salary details, start date, and contract term.
Degree certificate + ZAB / anabin recognition, required for Blue Card / Fachkraft; § 19c assessed by BA.
Professional experience proof, such as reference letters, social security / tax payment records; § 19c and IT specialist routes require >= 2 / 3 years.
Health insurance proof, including Schengen travel insurance before entry and switch to statutory GKV or private PKV after arrival.
Address proof, such as employer-provided temporary accommodation or own rental contract / Wohnungsgeberbestätigung.
German-language certificate if applicable: Anerkennungspartnerschaft A2; permanent residence A1 / B1.
Marriage certificate / child birth certificate if dependents accompany; Apostille + certified German translation required.
Visa application form, completed through the embassy or Auswärtiges Amt digital visa portal, + visa fee receipt €75.

2. Employer Documents

Company commercial register extract (Handelsregisterauszug, within 3 months).
Tax number (Steuernummer) + employer tax-registration proof.
Employment contract signed by the employer, including salary, working hours, leave, and social security.
Job description (Stellenbeschreibung) + explanation of hiring reason.
BA “Erklärung zum Beschäftigungsverhältnis” form, electronic version.
For shortage-occupation Blue Card / Fachkraft / § 19c: submit BA approval request.
From 2026.1.1: copy of written notice informing the employee of the free Faire Integration advisory right, recommended to archive for audit.

Tip: Qualification Recognition and Document Legalization

Germany is very strict on qualification recognition. Every non-German degree must first be assessed through ZAB (Zentralstelle für ausländisches Bildungswesen), and the result may be checked in the anabin database. Chinese university degrees must distinguish between “H+” (recognized) and non-“H+”. The former can proceed directly through Blue Card / Fachkraft; the latter may require additional qualification assessment or the § 19c experience route. Chinese academic / marriage / birth certificates must first obtain Apostille (simplified after China joined the Hague Convention in 2023.11) and then be translated into German by a vereidigter Übersetzer.

Detailed Steps

1.Employer and employee sign the employment contract. For shortage-occupation Blue Card / Fachkraft / § 19c experience routes, the employer must first submit “Erklärung zum Beschäftigungsverhältnis” to BA (BA approval approx. 1–4 weeks).
2.Employee submits the application through the Auswärtiges Amt digital visa portal in the responsible German embassy district (full coverage across 167 missions from 2026.2) and books the document / biometric appointment.
3.After receiving the application, the embassy sends it to the Ausländerbehörde at the employee’s future residence for joint review. Blue Card usually takes 2–6 weeks; other types 1–3 months.
4.After approval, employee collects a D national visa (national visa, valid for 90 days), allowing single or multiple entry to Germany.
5.Within 14 days after entering Germany, the employee must complete Anmeldung at the Bürgeramt / Einwohnermeldeamt using passport + entry visa + Wohnungsgeberbestätigung → obtain Meldebescheinigung and automatically receive Steuer-ID later.
6.Book an appointment with the Ausländerbehörde, provide biometrics, and apply for the physical residence permit eAT (elektronischer Aufenthaltstitel), usually mailed or collected after 4–6 weeks. The card records the visa type and validity.
7.Join statutory health insurance (GKV, e.g. TK / AOK / Barmer) or private insurance (PKV); after the employment contract starts, the employer withholds and pays contributions.

Tip: Shortage Occupations and Digital Visa Portal

“Shortage occupations (Engpassberufe / Mangelberufe)” determine the Blue Card salary threshold and permanent-residence route. BA publishes the list each year. In 2026 it includes IT, engineers (mechanical, electrical, civil), natural sciences, mathematics, doctors and nursing staff, teachers, etc. For shortage occupations, the salary threshold is reduced to €45,934.20 and BA usually gives tacit consent. The Auswärtiges Amt digital visa portal (Konsularportal) has been fully online since 2026.2.20; the whole process can be handled online except biometrics, significantly shortening the timeline.

Arrival Guide

Important Reminder: Anmeldung and eAT After Entry

After entering with a D visa, the employee may work immediately, but must complete Anmeldung (residence registration) within 14 days. Without Anmeldung, almost all subsequent matters such as bank account, social security, health insurance, tax number, and rental processing will be blocked. The physical eAT residence card must be completed within the initial 90-day visa validity.

1. Work-related

1.Within 14 days after entry: register at the local Bürgeramt and complete Anmeldung → obtain Meldebescheinigung + automatically receive Steuer-ID.
2.Book an appointment with the Ausländerbehörde to collect the physical eAT residence card; the card states residence type, validity, and additional work-permission notes.
3.Social security is automatically integrated into the German system: pension (RV), unemployment (AV), health insurance (KV), and long-term care (PV). Employee + employer contributions total ~40% of salary, about 20% each.
4.Personal income tax: based on 6 tax classes (Steuerklassen). 2026 basic allowance €12,096; top marginal rate 45% (annual income > €277,826), plus 5.5% solidarity surcharge for high earners and optional church tax.
5.Blue Card holders: continuous employment + pension contributions + “Leben in Deutschland” integration test; permanent residence (Niederlassungserlaubnis nach § 18c (2) AufenthG) possible after 21 months with B1 German or 27 months with A1 German.
6.On the first working day, the employer must provide written notice about the free Faire Integration advisory service (from 2026.1.1).

2. Daily Life

1.Mandatory health insurance: statutory GKV (mandatory for employees / monthly income <= €73,800) costs around 14.6% + 1.7% additional contribution, split with employer; high earners may choose private PKV.
2.Open a local bank account: Sparkasse, Deutsche Bank, Commerzbank, N26, ING-DiBa, Comdirect, etc.; Anmeldung + passport + residence permit required.
3.Housing: ImmoScout24, Immowelt, WG-Gesucht (shared flats). Berlin, Munich, Hamburg, and Frankfurt have tight rental markets; deposit (Kaution) usually 2–3 months cold rent.
4.SIM card: Telekom, Vodafone, O2, 1&1; prepaid / postpaid both available, passport + address required.
5.Public transport: Deutschlandticket (nationwide monthly pass €58/month; adjusted to €63 from 2026) covers metro, bus, and regional trains nationwide, but not ICE / IC / EC.
6.Mobile payments: cash remains common, while EC-Karte (Girocard) has the highest coverage; credit-card acceptance is lower than in other Western markets; Apple Pay / Google Pay / PayPal are common in cities and chains.
7.Broadcasting fee (Rundfunkbeitrag): every residence pays €18.36/month to ARD ZDF Deutschlandradio, regardless of whether the household owns a TV.

3. Dependent Visas

Spouse / registered partner + unmarried children under 18 may apply for family reunification (Familienzusammenführung). Family members of Blue Card / Fachkraft holders have open labour-market access after entry and do not need a separate work permit.
Spouse language requirement: A1 German in principle; spouses of Blue Card holders are exempt from A1, regardless of entry timing; nationals of the US / Canada / Japan / Korea / Australia / New Zealand / Israel, etc. are also exempt.
Parents / parents-in-law reunification from 2024.3.1: Blue Card / Fachkraft / Anerkennungspartnerschaft / Chancenkarte holders whose first residence permit was obtained on or after 2024.3.1 may bring their own and their spouse’s parents to Germany for long-term residence; sufficient living costs + health insurance must be proven.
Financial and insurance thresholds for family reunification: the employee’s income must cover the family’s living costs in Germany; Bürgergeld / social assistance must not be claimed.
The parents / parents-in-law reunification policy is currently valid until 2028.12.31.
Family members must also complete Anmeldung within 14 days after arrival and exchange for family eAT at the Ausländerbehörde.

Existing Residence Permit: Changing Employers

For the first 12 months after initial issuance, Germany’s EU Blue Card / Fachkraft residence permit is tied to the specific employer and position. During this period, changing employer requires prior approval from the Ausländerbehörde. After 12 months, the employee may change employer freely, but must still meet the conditions of the residence type and fulfil the notification duty.

1. Core Rules

Residence Permit TypeEmployer Change Rule
EU Blue Card (Blaue Karte EU)First 12 months: prior Ausländerbehörde consent required for employer change (approx. 2–4 weeks). After 12 months: only post-notification required; the new role must still meet the Blue Card salary threshold.
Fachkraft (§ 18a / 18b)First 12 months: ABH consent required, and the new role must meet BA approval conditions. After 12 months: notification only.
§ 19c (2) experience routeTied to employer and position throughout; employer change requires new BA approval + ABH consent.
Anerkennungspartnerschaft (§ 16d (3))Tied to the employer because the employer commits to supporting Anerkennung; employer change requires a new employer commitment + ABH consent.
Opportunity Card (Chancenkarte)No employer during job-search phase; once a job is found, must convert to another work residence permit (Blue Card / Fachkraft, etc.).
ICT intra-company transfer (§ 19)Tied to the sending company; cannot transfer into a German local company; may only be assigned among group companies within the EU.
Permanent residence (Niederlassungserlaubnis)Fully free; no employer or position restrictions.

2. Practical Steps to Change Employers

1.Employee signs an employment contract with the new employer. Blue Card holders confirm that the new position reaches the €50,700 (general) / €45,934.20 (shortage) threshold.
2.If within the first 12 months of the initial residence permit: employee submits an application for employer-change consent (Zustimmung zum Arbeitgeberwechsel) to the local Ausländerbehörde, attaching the new contract + new employer declaration + new job description.
3.For § 19c experience route or shortage-occupation Blue Card: the new employer also submits “Erklärung zum Beschäftigungsverhältnis” to BA.
4.Ausländerbehörde review takes approx. 2–4 weeks. The employee must not actually start work at the new employer before approval is granted.
5.Employer change after 12 months: employee may start directly and only needs to notify ABH in writing of the new employer information afterward, recommended within 4 weeks.
6.If the visa validity or type needs to change: apply for a new physical eAT residence card.
7.Blue Card unemployment tolerance: during unemployment, Blue Card holders retain residence rights for a “reasonable job-search period” (in practice usually 3–6 months), but must proactively notify ABH and prove active job search.

Important Reminder: Blue Card First 12 Months Employer and Position Binding

The Blue Card “first 12 months employer and position binding” rule is a core rule written into § 18g after the 2023.11 reform. During this period, any employer change or position adjustment, such as salary reduction or title change, requires prior written consent from the Ausländerbehörde. Otherwise, it may be treated as a breach of residence-permit conditions and may trigger withdrawal of the residence permit. Complete the consent procedure before the first day with the new employer.

4. Permanent Residence and Employer Change

Blue Card holders may apply for permanent residence (§ 18c (2)) after 21 months of legal work in Germany with B1 German or 27 months with A1 German, provided they have continuously paid pension contributions and passed the “Leben in Deutschland” integration test. Fachkraft holders usually need 33–48 months depending on language and social-security conditions. After permanent residence, there are no employer or position restrictions, and the employee may freely change jobs / become self-employed / start a business without affecting dependent residence.

Departure Guide

1.Submit resignation according to the employment contract notice period (Kündigungsfrist; statutory minimum 4 weeks to month-end, longer with seniority); employer completes final salary settlement + unused leave compensation (Urlaubsabgeltung).
2.Request an employment reference (Arbeitszeugnis), which German law protects as a neutral / positive employer evaluation.
3.Social-security notification: employer reports termination to the health insurer (GKV) within 6 weeks; employee should verify the “DEUV-Meldung” termination filing.
4.Tax return: file income tax return (Einkommensteuererklärung) for the departure year; over-withheld tax may be refunded; use Lohnsteuerhilfeverein or a tax advisor if needed.
5.Pension refund / social security: if Germany contributions are < 5 years and the home country has no bilateral social-security agreement with Germany, pension refund may be requested 24 months after departure. China and Germany have a bilateral social-security agreement, so contribution periods may be recognized or aggregated.
6.Complete Abmeldung (deregistration) at Bürgeramt; earliest 7 days before departure, using flight ticket or new address proof.
7.Cancel subscriptions: statutory health insurance (GKV) ends with employment; self-employed / private insurance must be cancelled in writing; phone / internet / rental contracts usually require 1–3 months notice.
8.Close German bank account: recommended to keep it for 1–2 months to settle final direct debits (rent, insurance, tax); clear all recurring payments before closing.
9.ARD ZDF broadcasting fee (Rundfunkbeitrag): stops automatically after Abmeldung; keep a copy of Abmeldung for records.
10.Return the physical eAT residence card if required by the local Ausländerbehörde; if future return is possible, keep the Steuer-ID (lifetime valid).

Key Policy Changes from 2023 to 2026

Germany passed the Skilled Workers Immigration Act (FEG) three-stage reform in 2023–2024 and continued digitalization and employer-compliance enforcement in 2026.
